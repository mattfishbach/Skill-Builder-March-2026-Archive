ABCDE Disrupting Thinking Worksheet

The ABCDE model comes from cognitive behavioral coaching. It helps you take control of difficult situations by interrupting automatic thinking patterns and choosing more productive responses.

The ABCDE Model

A Activating Event

What happened? Describe the situation as objectively as possible—as if you were a reporter with no prior knowledge of the facts.

Describe the event without interpretation or judgment...

Key: Stick to facts. "My colleague was promoted" not "My colleague unfairly got promoted."

B Beliefs

What did you believe about the event? What thoughts ran through your mind? These beliefs lead to your emotional response.

What assumptions or beliefs did you form about the situation?

Key: Be honest about what you actually thought, not what you think you "should" have thought.

C Consequences

What happened as a result of your beliefs? How did you feel? How did you behave? What was the impact on you and others?

Describe your emotional state and behavioral response...

Key: Include both emotional consequences (anger, resentment) and behavioral ones (confrontation, withdrawal).

D Disrupting Thought

Challenge your original beliefs. Think like an objective bystander. Is there another way to interpret the situation that could lead to a better outcome?

What alternative interpretation could replace your original belief?

Key: This isn't about being falsely positive—it's about finding a more useful and accurate way to interpret the situation.

E Effect

If you adopted the disrupting thought, how would you feel differently? How would you behave differently? What would the new outcome be?

Describe the new emotional state and behavioral response...

Key: Focus on concrete changes—not just "I'd feel better" but what you'd actually do differently.

Example: Priya and Dan

Before (A → B → C)

A: Dan is late with weekly information again.

B: "Dan deliberately disrespects me. He doesn't think my work matters."

C: Priya feels angry, confronts Dan aggressively, starts an argument, damages the relationship further.

After (D → E)

D: "Maybe Dan doesn't realize how his lateness impacts me. Maybe he doesn't see the relevance of the information."

E: Priya feels less angry, has a calm conversation with Dan about the impact, they find a collaborative solution together.

Questions to Generate Disrupting Thoughts

  • Am I seeing the full picture, or just my perspective?
  • Would an objective bystander interpret this the same way I did?
  • Is there a more charitable explanation for the other person's behavior?
  • What would I advise a friend who described this situation to me?
  • Will this matter in a week? A month? A year?
  • What do I actually want to achieve here—and will my current response get me there?
Remember: The key is to repeat this process until it feels comfortable. Over time, you'll start naturally challenging your automatic beliefs before they drive your behavior. This is one of the most powerful tools for building emotional intelligence.
