Access Control Best Practices

Access control is your first line of defense. These principles help ensure the right people have the right access at the right time—and no more.

Core Principles

1. Principle of Least Privilege

Users should have only the minimum access necessary to do their job.

  • Start with zero access and add only what's needed
  • Review access regularly and remove what's no longer required
  • Separate admin accounts from daily-use accounts
  • Just-in-time access for elevated privileges

2. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Require at least two forms of verification for all access.

  • Something you know (password)
  • Something you have (phone, security key)
  • Something you are (fingerprint, face)
  • MFA blocks 99% of automated attacks (Microsoft)

3. Account Lifecycle Management

Manage accounts from creation to deletion.

  • Provision access only after proper approval
  • Review access when roles change
  • Deprovision immediately when access ends
  • Audit dormant accounts regularly

4. Individual Accountability

Every action should be traceable to a specific person.

  • No shared accounts or credentials
  • Unique usernames for each individual
  • Comprehensive logging of all access
  • Regular review of access logs

5. Zero Trust

Never trust, always verify—regardless of location.

  • Verify every request, every time
  • Assume breach and limit blast radius
  • Continuous authentication, not just at login
  • Network location doesn't equal trust

Common Access Control Failures

Zombie Accounts

Accounts that remain active after users leave or change roles. The Colonial Pipeline breach started with a forgotten VPN account.

Privilege Creep

Access accumulates over time as people change roles but never lose old permissions. Results in users with far more access than they need.

Shared Credentials

Multiple people using the same account. Destroys accountability and makes MFA ineffective.

Case Study: Colonial Pipeline (2021)

A single compromised VPN account—inactive and without MFA—led to a ransomware attack that shut down the largest fuel pipeline in the US. The account belonged to an employee who had left the company but was never deprovisioned.

Lesson: Account lifecycle management and MFA aren't optional.
