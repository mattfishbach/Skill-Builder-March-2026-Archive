5-Step Stress Response Guide

When emotions rise quickly, use this 5-step process to slow down your reaction and choose a response that serves you better.

1

Identify the Emotional Reaction

Take a step back and recognize when you're reacting emotionally rather than objectively. This awareness is one of the most effective tactics you can employ.

Notice: "I'm feeling angry right now and I might not be thinking clearly."
Immediately act on the emotion without pausing.
2

Remove Yourself from the Situation

Unless it's imperative that you react immediately (a genuine crisis), take 60 seconds to step away. Remove yourself physically from the situation and take deep breaths.

Say "I need a moment" and step out for 60 seconds.
Fire off an email or confront someone while emotions are high.

Why it works: Slow, deep breaths lower your heart rate and reduce physiological symptoms like sweating and blushing.

3

Give Yourself Time to Recover

Let your body recover. Don't make decisions and don't keep replaying the scenario in your head. Focus on feeling more level, both emotionally and physically.

Focus on calming down—take a short walk, get water, breathe.
Ruminate on the situation or start planning your response while still upset.
4

Challenge Your Thoughts

Is there a way to think about the situation that creates a better outcome? Although you may feel strongly about confronting the issue, consider whether that's really the best option in the long run.

Ask: "Is my interpretation the only one? What would an objective observer see?"
Assume your initial emotional interpretation is the only valid one.
5

Choose How to Respond

You may decide that your original thoughts were the best course of action, or you may decide on another approach. Either way, you've now consciously assessed the best option rather than reacting automatically.

Make a deliberate choice based on what will achieve the best outcome.
Default to your first impulse without considering alternatives.

Perspective Shifting

Building a Broader Perspective

Part of managing yourself is learning to see situations from multiple viewpoints. Tips:

  • Seek different points of view — See contrasting ideas as a bonus, not a nuisance
  • Ask more questions — Listen to how others see the world
  • Spend time with new people — Break out of your usual peer group
  • Read broadly — Expand your horizons through diverse topics

Remember: you don't have to change your opinion, but understanding alternative viewpoints helps you communicate more effectively and find innovative solutions.

Keep it simple: The more simple your strategy for slowing down (breathing, walking, drinking water), the easier it is to use in the heat of the moment. Start with one technique and practice it until it becomes automatic.
