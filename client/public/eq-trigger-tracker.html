The key insight: it's not the event itself that causes your reaction—it's how you think and feel about the event. This is where you can intervene.

What Is a Trigger?

Definition

A trigger is an event, situation, or thought that consistently takes you in the wrong direction—causing you to think and react in a way that isn't helpful and won't achieve the best outcome.

Common signs you've been triggered:

  • You react with disproportionate intensity
  • You experience physiological symptoms (racing heart, sweating, face flushing)
  • You recognize the pattern afterward: "I always react this way when..."
  • You regret your response later

What Is a Cognitive Hijack?

Definition

A cognitive hijack occurs when a trigger sets off an irrational emotional response that bypasses your rational thinking. Your automatic processing "gets it wrong"—making assumptions and jumping to conclusions without checking the facts.

The result is a behavioral response that doesn't serve your best interest.

Find the Patterns

Look for Themes

Review your 3 situations and ask yourself:

  • Do the same types of events keep triggering you? (e.g., receiving feedback, feeling excluded, time pressure)
  • Do you notice the same emotional response each time? (e.g., anger, defensiveness, withdrawal)
  • Are there common assumptions you make? (e.g., "They don't respect me," "I'm not good enough")
  • Do your physiological responses follow a pattern?
Example: "I notice that every time someone questions my work in a meeting, I feel my face get hot and I immediately get defensive. My assumption is always that they're criticizing me personally, not my work. I tend to respond by justifying everything instead of listening."
Top Tip: Practice this reflection frequently after facing a challenging situation. It's this reflection time that will help you build your emotional intelligence over time. The more aware you are of your triggers, the better equipped you'll be to intercept the response before it hijacks your behavior.
